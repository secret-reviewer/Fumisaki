Rider Log Entry #003

2023: The 6,000km Redemption

Returning to Thailand over a decade later. From mechanical failures and flat tires to train-hopping through the heart of the country.

The 12-Day Survival Log

Day 1-2
SG ➔ Hatyai ➔ Chumphon (Battling tire leaks & overheating)
Day 3
Chumphon ➔ Bang Yai (Accident with local car; 8k Baht damage)
Day 4-5
Bang Yai ➔ Phitsanulok ➔ Chiang Mai (Exhaustion sets in)
Day 6
Chiang Mai Rest Day (Full bike service & Khao Soy)
Day 7
Chiang Mai ➔ Mae Hong Son (The Doi Inthanon 1000-corner grind)
Day 8
MHS ➔ Pai (1864 Corners Certification & Stargazing)
Day 9-10
Pai ➔ Chiang Mai ➔ BKK (Train-hop from hell: 3rd Class)
Day 11-12
Hatyai ➔ Ipoh ➔ Home (The final push back to SG)

The "Shag" Reality

This trip was a test of patience. We faced tire leaks in Chumphon, a minor accident in Bang Yai that cost us 8,000 Baht (reminder: buy COMPREHENSIVE insurance), and the sheer physical toll of Doi Inthanon's 45-degree slopes.

One of the most intense moments was the return leg via the Thai State Railway. We took 3rd Class from Bangkok to Hatyai—no aircon, bugs flying through open windows, and doors that never close. It wasn't sleep; it was a 15-hour survival exercise.

Hard-Earned Lessons

1. Group Safety & Communication

Mirror checks are mandatory. We almost lost a bike because one rider didn't notice the rear. "Chin chye" (anything) is okay for dinner, but on the road, discipline is the only thing that keeps you safe.

2. Mechanical Prep

We found 8L of oil in a gearbox after an overheat because we top up along the way. Always check your bike at a trusted workshop weeks—and days—before moving off. Carry a jump starter, tire repair kit, and CO2 cylinders.

3. Digital Nomads on Wheels

If you're a content creator, get a FAST SD card reader. Spending 8 hours overnight transferring footage on a slow reader is a mistake you only make once (me) :). Also, install a wireless phone charger; GPS drains batteries faster than you think.

Closing Thoughts

6,000km later, we made it back. From the waterfalls of Wachirathan to the night markets of Pai, the journey was worth every drop of sweat and every Baht spent. But remember: The road condition in Thailand and the NSH are totally different beasts. Ride defensively, or don't ride at all.

Rider Log Entry #002

The 14-Day Rush: Laos & MHS

A "touch-and-go" mission across four borders. 14 days of field-camp intensity, winding Laos mountain passes, and the brutal reality of the road.

The Impossible Ambition

In June 2013, we planned a trip that wasn't for the faint-hearted. The objective: SG ➔ Malaysia ➔ Thailand ➔ Laos ➔ Mae Hong Son (MHS) ➔ SG. A 14-day loop where rest was a luxury and the schedule was "very rush."

The Plan (The 4,000km+ Grind)

Day 1-2
SG to Hatyai Border
Day 3
Hatyai to Hua Hin (760km)
Day 4-6
Hua Hin to Nong Khai (TH) ➔ Vang Vieng (Laos)
Day 7-8
Plains of Jars ➔ Luang Prabang ➔ China Border
Day 9-11
Chiang Mai ➔ Pai ➔ Mae Hong Son (1864 Corners)
Day 12-14
Tak ➔ Bangkok ➔ Night Train ➔ KL ➔ SG

Rider Requirements

To survive this, I set strict rules. Riders had to be "troopers"—capable of fixing punctures, riding at night, ignoring the weather, and staying mentally sharp despite 14 days of "no sleep" feelings. No "young and dangerous" attitudes; only the disciplined would survive.

The Reality Check: Day 2

The trip was cursed from the start. My bike struggled with RPM issues, requiring a carb wash on the roadside. My GPS charging failed, leaving me blind. But the real blow came near Alor Setar.

A communication breakdown during a fuel stop led to a high-speed collision. I crashed hard. My fairings shattered, signal lights snapped, and my armor was shredded. My helmet—my expensive full-face—was scratched to hell. I didn't hit the rider; I threw my gloves in frustration, knowing the physical and mental damage was done.

Lessons from the Asphalt

The mission was noble—we were carrying funds and goodies for an orphanage in Laos—but the road had other plans. I had to make the hardest call: Withdraw.

While the rest pushed on, I retreated to Ipoh, battling nightmares and technical failures. It was a humbling reminder that on a long tour, your gear and your group's communication are the only things keeping you out of the ditch.

Key Takeaways:

  • Group Dynamics: Rear-view mirrors aren't for display; they are lifelines. If you can't see your buddies, you shouldn't be leading.
  • Mental Health: A crash doesn't just break the bike; it breaks the spirit. Know when to turn back.
  • Brotherhood: Deepest thanks to the buddy in Ipoh who opened his home when I was at my lowest.

Rider Log Entry #001

Malaysia ➔ Thailand: The Hatyai Run

Back in 2012, crossing the border on two wheels was more than just a trip—it was a rite of passage. Here is the breakdown of the 4-day grind.

The Mission Timeline

Day 1: The Midnight Roll-out

We met at West Coast McDonald's at 2:00 AM. By 3:15 AM, the engines roared to life. We maintained a steady 90-100km/h pace across the North-South Highway. By 5:00 PM, we hit the Sadao border. Exhausted but hyped, we settled in for the night.

Day 2: Local Customisation

No riding today. We hit the town in Tuk-Tuks. This was the day for bike TLC—getting seats wrapped and custom Thailand number plates made. We capped it off with legendary local massages.

Day 3: Coastal Exploration

Rode out to Ko Yo in Songkhla. The scenery was breathtaking, a stark contrast to the concrete jungle of Singapore. The "Old Hatyai" vibes were strong.

Day 4: The Rain & The Return

Nature decided to test us. We left at 10:00 AM under a massive downpour. It didn't stop. With full rain gear on, we pushed through, finally reaching back to Singapore around midnight.

Essential Kit List

  • Self-Sufficiency: Tyre repair kits (with gas), spare spark plugs, and clutch cables.
  • Security: 1-meter heavy-duty chain and disc locks. You can't "guard" a bike 24/7.
  • Pure Grit: Waterproof bags and wind-proof lighters for those roadside breaks.

Rider's Reflections

Singapore and the NSH are two completely different worlds. On this trip, we faced everything: from bombing scares (that turned out to be a loud short circuit) to dodging micro-sleep on the return leg.

Whether you're on a Class 2 monster or a 2B KRR, the road treats everyone the same. Expect the unexpected, because things usually don't go your way—and that's where the fun is.
