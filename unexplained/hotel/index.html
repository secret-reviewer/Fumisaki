Touring Log #001

The Two Hotels That Got Worse

Long-distance touring teaches you that the cheapest hotels often leave the strongest memories. Thailand gave us two — each stranger than the last — and by the second one, nobody was pretending to be brave anymore.

// SR-TOUR-001 // Thailand Touring // 380 Baht & The Mountain

The 380-Baht Hotel

During long-distance touring, riders eventually learn one thing: the cheapest hotels create the strongest memories. This night came after an already draining stretch — we had been through an accident the day before, so everyone was running on empty. Body tired. Mind tired. Bikes dirty. Mood heavy. By the time we found a place, nobody cared about quality. 380 baht. Cheap enough that we accepted without thinking much.

At first glance, the place looked less like a hotel and more like an old army barrack. Long corridors. Plain walls. Dim lighting. Even climbing the stairs felt familiar in an uncomfortable way — it instantly reminded me of Tekong during NS.

. . .

The moment we entered the room, something felt wrong. Not dirty. Not obviously haunted-looking. Just wrong. The room was unusually large for such a cheap hotel. Too much empty space. That kind of silence where even your own footsteps sound unnaturally loud.

We switched on the TV immediately. Then all the room lights. Nobody suggested turning anything off afterward. That itself already said enough.

. . .

At one point I stepped outside alone for air. The corridor was dim and completely empty. No footsteps. No voices. Just the distant humming of old building lights.

And standing there, I suddenly had this overwhelming feeling that something was nearby. Not visible. Not dramatic. Just the strong instinct that I shouldn't stay outside too long.

I went straight back inside and closed the door.

That night, none of us switched off the lights. The TV stayed on until morning. And despite being physically exhausted, sleep never felt deep.

The Mountain Hotel

That 380-baht place was strange enough. But there was another hotel later in the same trip that turned out even worse — bad enough that all of us ended up sleeping together in the same room just to get through the night.

We didn't choose it because it looked good. We chose it because it was the nearest available place after riding past nightfall. And once night fully settles on those mountain roads in Thailand, everything changes. The route leading in was almost completely dark. No streetlamps. No shops. No proper signboards.

Even finding the hotel entrance was difficult. When we finally entered the compound, we couldn't immediately find the owner — he was sitting somewhere deeper inside the darkness with a few other guests. That alone already felt unsettling.

. . .

After some awkward communication, we were told only two rooms were left. But the rooms were separated far apart. One was located further inside the compound, deeper into the darkness. The other was nearer to a wooden fence near the entrance.

The moment we heard that, both of us made the same decision without discussion.

One room. Together. No point acting brave.

. . .

The room itself barely felt like a proper hotel. Old structure — concrete, wood panels, metal roofing. The kind where every movement creates sound. The TV was a CRT — huge fat back, tiny screen. But it became comforting. Not for watching. Just for noise. Something to push against the silence outside.

. . .

Then we discovered the toilet. To reach it, we had to lower our heads through a small gate-like opening, then step partially outside into darkness before reaching the toilet a few steps away. Beyond that — just wilderness. No proper walls. Just darkness and nature.

Nobody wanted to go alone.

. . .

I was knocked out from exhaustion and didn't hear anything that night. Only the next morning did my riding buddy tell me what happened.

Sometime very late, the electricity tripped. The room went dark. Then the old CRT turned to static — that loud, old-school crackling sound. He woke up, found the switch, restored power, then quickly turned the TV off.

And according to him — the moment the electricity came back on, everything suddenly felt normal again. No more strange atmosphere. No more uneasy feeling. Like whatever had been pressing against the room had decided to leave when the lights returned.

What Both Hotels Had in Common

Till today, I still don't know whether either experience was just exhaustion and unfamiliar surroundings, or something the walls of those places were holding from before we arrived. Both explanations feel possible. Both feel incomplete.

But one detail from the mountain hotel still sticks. Out of all the rooms available that night — neither of us chose the one deeper inside the darkness. We made that decision instantly, without discussion, without needing to explain it to each other. Some choices don't need reasoning. The instinct is fast enough on its own.

Hotel 1
380 baht — barrack-style, Thailand. Lights left on all night.
Hotel 2

Incident
Power trip late at night. CRT static. Atmosphere normalised upon power restoration.

Log Classification: Unexplained / Touring / Accommodation

Touring Log #002

The Hotel That Felt Too Familiar

We arrived late. The compound looked almost abandoned. A woman appeared from the darkness without making a sound. And the moment I stepped into my room, I already knew where everything was — before I'd ever been there.

// SR-TOUR-002 // Thailand Touring // Familiarity Log

Arriving Late

We arrived much later than planned. By the time we entered the area, the roads were already empty. Most nearby shops had closed. The deeper we rode in, the quieter everything became — no passing traffic, no people sitting outside, not even the stray dogs that usually bark at incoming bikes.

The hotel was somewhere near the mountain area, chosen mainly because it was the nearest available place before nightfall made riding too dangerous. When we reached the entrance, the place looked almost abandoned. A few dim yellow lights. The reception area empty. We pressed the bell once. Then again. Still nothing. After almost twenty minutes of waiting in complete silence — the only sound the metallic ticking of our bikes cooling down — the atmosphere had already shifted.

The Woman From the Darkness

Not threatening. Just strangely still.

. . .

I remember standing there, looking deeper into the compound, when I suddenly felt something I still can't explain.

Familiarity.

Not the kind where you recognise a place from photos online. The uncomfortable kind — where your mind already knows the environment before you've explored it. Even before walking further in, I somehow knew there was another section behind the reception building. I knew the corridor on the left led toward the rooms. At the time I convinced myself it was just because roadside hotels share similar layouts.

. . .

Then, without any footsteps or doors opening, a woman appeared from somewhere deeper inside the compound. She didn't look surprised to see us waiting. No apology. No confusion. She simply asked whether we needed rooms — her voice calm, almost as if she had expected us to arrive.

The Room I Somehow Already Knew

She showed us a few available rooms. The one nearest to the carpark immediately caught my attention — slightly more expensive, but from the window I had a direct view of my bike below. As a rider, that matters more than room size sometimes. I took it without hesitation.

. . .

The strange feeling became stronger the moment I stepped inside. Again, not fear. The opposite.

Comfort. The room felt familiar in a way that made no sense.

I knew where the switches were before reaching for them. I placed my riding gear down naturally, like I had done it there before. Moving around the room felt automatic. No adjustment period. No "first-time" feeling. Just an overwhelming sense that I had returned somewhere, rather than arrived somewhere new.

. . .

The weirdest part is that nothing paranormal happened that night. No shadows. No strange noises. No sightings. Everything was peaceful. We slept well, woke up normally, and continued our journey the next morning without any issues.

Why It Still Stays With Me

Out of every hotel I've stayed at across all my touring trips, that is still the only place that never felt unfamiliar to me. Not even on arrival. Not even in the first few seconds.

I've tried to explain it rationally — similar layouts, exhaustion warping perception, pattern recognition from years of budget hotels. All possible. None of them fully account for knowing where the light switches were before I reached for them. Or for the settled, returned feeling that was there from the moment I stepped through the door.

Some places feel like you've been there before. This one felt like I had left something there and was coming back to collect it. I still don't know what that means. And honestly, I'm not sure I want to.

// SIGNAL RECORDED: Strong spatial familiarity upon arrival at a never-visited location. Rider knew switch locations instinctively. No paranormal incident occurred. Feeling of return rather than arrival. Only hotel across all touring trips to produce this effect.

Additional Field Notes: The Hotel That Felt Too Familiar

Log Classification: Unexplained / Déjà Vu / Touring Encounter

[SPATIAL FAMILIARITY] Standard déjà vu is brief and disorienting. What is described here is sustained, functional familiarity — accurate spatial knowledge of a never-visited location. Knowing where light switches are before reaching for them is not consistent with normal déjà vu. It suggests either forgotten prior exposure, or something else without a straightforward explanation.

[THE WOMAN'S ARRIVAL] Appearing without footsteps or door sounds, showing no surprise after 20 minutes of unanswered bells, and speaking with the calm expectation of someone who knew guests were coming — individually explainable, together harder to dismiss.

[WHY NOTHING HAPPENED] Not all unexplained experiences are threatening. Some locations carry a feeling of recognition that is unsettling precisely because it is comfortable. The discomfort comes from the impossibility of the recognition itself, not from any danger.

INCIDENT ASSESSMENT
  • Type: Sustained spatial familiarity — never visited location
  • Threat level: None. Peaceful stay, normal departure.
  • Classification: Unexplained — not hostile, not resolved.
