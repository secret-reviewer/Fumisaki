Unexplained Log #003

The Sudden Passenger

It was past midnight. The roads were empty. I was riding alone — or at least, I thought I was. The pillion stand was down. I hadn't noticed.

🏍️
// SR-UNX-003 // Midnight // Singapore Roads

Context

Every rider knows the pillion footrest. You fold it up when you're solo — it's just habit. But that night I'd parked up earlier, had a friend sit behind briefly for a photo, and when he hopped off, neither of us bothered to fold it back up. Small thing. Forgotten.

I left solo. Pillion stand still extended. The bike — without knowing it — was still configured to welcome someone.

What Happened

Five, maybe ten minutes into the ride, the bike started feeling different.

. . .

Not mechanically. The engine was fine. RPM steady. Speed wasn't climbing unusually. But there was a weight — a drag — a resistance that wasn't there when I left.

I checked my mirrors instinctively. Nothing.

But the feeling stayed.

. . .

Then the screaming started.

Not a person. The bike. The engine note shifted — not like it was revving harder, but like it was straining against something. Like it was carrying a load it hadn't been told about. RPM and speed were both reading normal, but the sound… wasn't right. It was working too hard for the numbers it was showing.

I've ridden long enough to know when a bike sounds wrong. This was wrong.

. . .

I pulled over at the next stretch of road shoulder. Killed the engine.

Sat there. Looked behind me.

Nothing.

No person. No object. Nothing physically there.

That's when I noticed the pillion stand — still down. Open. Extended like an invitation.

I don't know what came over me, but I spoke out loud. Just a few words. Something along the lines of — I'm sorry, I didn't know you were there. This ride's done. You need to go now.

I folded the stand up.

Started the engine.

. . .

The bike felt lighter immediately. The engine note returned to normal — clean, smooth, nothing extra in it. The resistance was gone. Whatever weight I'd been carrying, it wasn't there anymore.

I rode home without incident.

Rider's Note

I've heard this story from other riders before. It's one of those things that gets passed around in kopitiam conversations — fold up your pillion stand when you're riding solo, especially at night. Not because of aerodynamics. For another reason entirely. I always thought it was superstition.

I don't call it that anymore.

// SIGNAL RECORDED: Engine behaviour returned to baseline immediately after pillion stand was folded and verbal acknowledgement was made. No mechanical cause was identified before or after the incident.

Additional Field Notes: The Sudden Passenger

Log Classification: Unexplained / Mechanical Anomaly / Road Encounter

[PILLION STAND LORE] Among older riders in Malaysia and Singapore, there is a well-known practice: never ride with your pillion stand down unless you have a pillion. The belief is that an extended stand signals vacancy — and on certain roads, at certain hours, something may decide to fill that space.

[ENGINE BEHAVIOUR] Under normal load changes, RPM and speed adjust together. A situation where the engine sounds strained but instruments read normal is not a standard mechanical failure pattern. It suggests the load was not physical in any conventional sense.

[THE APOLOGY] Multiple accounts from riders across Southeast Asia describe the same resolution — acknowledging the presence, apologising for the inconvenience, and asking it to leave. Not commanding. Not panicking. Just speaking plainly. It consistently works.

INCIDENT ASSESSMENT
  • Mechanical cause: None identified
  • Duration: 5–10 minutes of abnormal behaviour
  • Resolution method: Verbal + physical (pillion stand folded)
Unexplained Log #002

The Angel That Brought Me Home

Micro-sleep on a bike is a death sentence. Most riders who've hit that wall will tell you about the accidents that followed. I didn't get an accident. I got something else.

🛣️
// SR-UNX-002 // Long Haul // Guardian

The Danger Most Riders Know

Any rider who has done long distances — especially overnight runs — knows the feeling. It starts subtle. Your eyes get heavy for a fraction of a second. You snap back. You think you're fine. Then it happens again, slightly longer. Then again.

Micro-sleep is insidious because you don't know it's happening. One moment you're riding. Then there's a gap. Then you're riding again, slightly further down the road, with no memory of the in-between. On a bike at 90km/h, a two-second gap is 50 metres of uncontrolled machine.

I've heard the stories. Rider found unconscious in a ditch. Rider who woke up already on the gravel shoulder with no idea how he got there. Rider who didn't wake up at all. We all know someone who knows someone.

That night, I was in the same trap.

What I Remember

It was a long ride back. Hours of highway. Past 2am. I was running on whatever reserves I had left, and they were running out.

. . .

I felt the weight on my eyelids. The familiar pull. I was fighting it — sitting up straighter, moving my head, trying to stay sharp.

Then — nothing.

. . .

Then — I was still on the road. Still moving. Still in my lane.

But I wasn't alone anymore.

. . .

There was a presence behind me. I can't describe it better than that — a presence. Not a passenger I'd let on. Not a feeling I'd had before. Something behind me, steady, calm. Not threatening. The opposite of threatening. It felt like being guided. Like having a hand on the back of your shoulder, keeping you upright when you'd otherwise list sideways.

I don't know how much time passed. But I was alert. Fully, completely alert in a way that made no sense given how exhausted I'd been moments before. The roads were clear. The bike was smooth. I was home before I'd processed what had happened.

. . .

I pulled into my carpark. Cut the engine.

Sat for a moment.

Said thank you. Out loud. To whatever had been there.

When I looked behind me — nothing.

Not a person. Not a shadow. The seat was empty.

But the ride was done, and I was home, and I was alive.

Felt behind rider — calming, stabilising, non-threatening

Remained in lane, no incident, arrived home safely

After verbal thanks — presence was gone

What Other Riders Say

This is not a unique account. Dig into any forum with long-distance riders across Southeast Asia and you'll find versions of this story. The presence. The sudden alertness. The inexplicable safety after a moment that should have ended badly. Some riders call it luck. Some call it instinct.

Some call it exactly what it felt like — something that was there when it shouldn't have been, helping when it didn't have to.

I'm not going to put a name to it. I'm just going to say it happened, it was real, and I'm grateful.

// SIGNAL RECORDED: Micro-sleep onset confirmed. Rider arrived home safely with no memory of the critical gap. Presence felt throughout — departed upon acknowledgement. No rational explanation found.

The Practical Note

Whatever you believe — don't push it. If you're tired enough to micro-sleep, pull over. Petrol station. Road shoulder. Carpark. Rest. No destination is worth that gap in consciousness on two wheels. I was fortunate. Not everyone gets what I got that night.

Rest when the road tells you to. And if something ever keeps you upright when you shouldn't be — say thank you.

Additional Field Notes: The Angel That Brought Me Home

Log Classification: Unexplained / Protective Encounter / Long Distance

[MICRO-SLEEP SCIENCE] A micro-sleep episode lasting 2–3 seconds at highway speed covers 50–80 metres with no rider input. On a motorcycle, this typically results in a lane drift or loss of control. Arriving safely in lane after a confirmed micro-sleep event with no corrective action taken by the rider is statistically unusual.

[THE ACCOUNTS] Across rider communities in Malaysia, Thailand, and Singapore, stories of protective presences on long-distance night rides appear with enough frequency that they are not considered isolated incidents. The consistent elements: sudden full alertness after a fatigue collapse, a felt presence, safe arrival, and departure of that presence upon thanks.

[THE ADVICE] Regardless of belief, the rider community consensus remains unchanged — pull over when tired. Whatever protection may exist on the road, it is not a reason to push limits. It is a reminder that some forces take care of you when you cannot care for yourself, and you owe them the respect of not making that necessary.

INCIDENT ASSESSMENT
  • Risk level at onset: Critical — confirmed micro-sleep while riding
  • Expected outcome: Loss of control event
  • Actual outcome: Safe arrival, no incident
Unexplained Log #001

Punggol, Before It Became Punggol

This was before everything was built up. No HDB. No lights. No people. Just one long stretch of road — and the fishing spot at the end that people said you might not feel like leaving.

🎣
// SR-UNX-001 // Punggol End // Old Singapore

The Place

Old-timers who knew Punggol before the development will understand what I'm describing. There was a version of Punggol that no longer exists — a dark, quiet stretch of land at the northeastern edge of Singapore. Kampung houses. Dirt paths. And at the very end of it all, a fishing spot that sat right at the water's edge.

The road in was long and straight and unlit. No streetlamps in those days. You drove or rode in on faith and whatever headlights you had, and the darkness on either side of you was complete.

People talked about that place. Not loudly — but they talked. The specific thing they said was this: if you go there late enough, you won't feel like walking back.

I heard it from a few different people. Old uncles, a couple of older riders. Always said the same way — not as a warning exactly, more like a fact. The way you'd say: don't lean into a corner if you haven't checked your tyres. Just something you know.

I didn't believe it.

So one night, I went anyway.

Going In

I rode in late. Well past the hour where anyone sensible would be there.

The road was exactly as described — long, straight, empty. My headlight carved a tunnel through the dark. The trees on either side were old growth, thick canopy, the kind that blocks even starlight.

Didn't think much of it at first. Same as any other quiet place, I told myself. Just darker.

I parked at the end. Walked to the water. Sat there.

. . .

Nothing happened.

No sounds. No movement. Not even wind.

That was the strange part.

. . .

A spot near water at night — even calm water — should have something. Frogs. Insects. The sound of the tide. The ordinary background noise of a living place.

There was none of that.

It felt paused.

Like someone had pressed mute on everything, and the world was holding its breath, and I was the only thing in it still moving.

I sat there for a while. Telling myself I was fine. Telling myself it was just quiet. Enjoying the solitude.

But the solitude didn't feel empty.

It felt occupied.

Going Out

After a while I stood up to leave.

That's when it started.

. . .

The path back looked the same. Same dirt, same trees, same dark. But it felt longer than when I'd walked in. Not physically — my legs knew the distance was the same. But something in the walk had changed. Like the space had stretched around me without moving.

I slowed down without deciding to.

My feet were still moving. I hadn't stopped. I hadn't frozen up. But my pace had dropped to something careful and deliberate, the way you move when every part of you is paying attention to something you can't see.

. . .

I didn't run. I want to be clear about that. There was no panic. No moment of terror that sent me sprinting. Just this steady, conscious awareness that I needed to finish the walk — that it was something to be completed, not something to be fled from.

Step by step.

The whole way out felt like that. Like something I was being permitted to do, rather than something I was choosing.

. . .

When I reached the main road, everything went back to normal.

Cars. Streetlights. Sound returning. The world un-muting itself all at once.

I stood at the edge for a moment. Looked back down the dark road.

Didn't go back in.

Rode home. Didn't stop.

Location
Punggol End — old Singapore, pre-development era
Time
Late night — well past midnight
Going in
Normal. Quiet. Nothing unusual.
At the spot
Total silence. No ambient sound. Felt occupied, not empty.
Going out
Path felt longer. Pace slowed involuntarily. Sense of being permitted to leave.
On main road
Everything normalised immediately — sound, light, atmosphere.
Return visits
None. Not at that hour.

What I Understand Now

I went in a sceptic. I came out something more complicated than that.

I'm not going to tell you what was there. I don't know what was there. What I know is that the place had a quality to it — a specific, felt quality — that I have not experienced anywhere else. And I understand now, with a clarity I didn't have before I went, exactly why those old uncles said what they said the way they said it.

Not a ghost story. Not a warning designed to scare. Just a fact about a place — stated plainly by people who'd been there and come back and knew.

Punggol End is gone now. Built over. Lit up. Populated. Whatever was there — if it was ever just one thing — has either moved on or been buried under concrete and HDB blocks and a waterway park.

Maybe that's fine. Maybe it's better that way.

But on certain quiet nights, when I'm riding through that part of the island and the road gets briefly dark between streetlamps, I still think about that walk out. And I still feel the same thing I felt then.

That some places hold something. That you don't always know what it is. And that sometimes the most honest thing you can say is: I went. I felt it. I came back. And I never went at that hour again.

// SIGNAL RECORDED: Complete absence of ambient sound at waterfront location. Involuntary change in movement pattern during exit. Sudden normalisation upon reaching main road. No rational environmental explanation for the silence or the altered perception of distance.

Additional Field Notes: Punggol End

Log Classification: Unexplained / Location / Old Singapore

[PUNGGOL HISTORY] Before Singapore's rapid northeastern development in the 2000s, Punggol was largely undeveloped — a mix of former kampung land, seafront areas, and old fishing grounds. Punggol End specifically referred to the far northeastern tip, known as a fishing destination and, among older residents, associated with unexplained phenomena at night. The area is now fully developed.

[THE SILENCE] A waterfront location with no ambient sound — no insects, no water movement, no wildlife — is ecologically abnormal. Animals and insects go silent in response to threat or disturbance. Sustained total silence suggests something in that environment was causing sustained suppression of normal activity. What that was, is not recorded.

[INVOLUNTARY PACE CHANGE] Slowing down without conscious decision in a context of no physical obstruction points to something affecting perception or movement at a level below conscious control. Whether environmental, psychological, or otherwise — the effect was consistent with accounts from others who had visited the same location at similar hours.

INCIDENT ASSESSMENT
  • Location: No longer accessible in original form
  • Corroborating accounts: Multiple — consistent across different riders and residents
  • Recommendation: Some places are not meant to be tested. Go at daylight if curiosity demands it. Not at night.
Unexplained Log #004

The Route That Wouldn't End

Back then, entering Malaysia was different. No live maps. No reliable signal. You rode on memory — and that night, memory wasn't enough to get us out.

🧭
// SR-UNX-004 // JB Night Ride // Nokia 6280

How It Started

Back then, crossing into Malaysia meant riding on instinct. No realtime traffic. No proper mobile signal everywhere. The phone I had at the time was a Nokia 6280 — sliding phone, small screen. GPS wasn't even reliably functional yet. Half the time the map froze, the signal drifted, or the arrow jumped around like it was confused itself.

That night, sometime past 1am, two of us crossed in with a simple plan — head to the old bak kut teh lane. The usual biker spot. Shops only really alive after evening, and late-night JB rides were completely normal back then.

Petrol was full. Roads mostly empty. Cool night air. Just two riders following old memories.

Where It Went Wrong

Somewhere after a wrong turn, things started feeling off.

The roads became narrower. Streetlights fewer. The surroundings looked older.

We joked at first. "Eh bro, you sure or not?"

. . .

But slowly, even the jokes stopped.

Then we reached somewhere neither of us had ever been before.

A graveyard. Not the modern type. The old quiet kind. Dark. Uneven ground. Trees moving from the wind.

. . .

That was when we finally switched on the GPS. The Nokia took forever to load. Screen kept freezing. Signal bar dropping in and out.

Then: Route found.

Relieved, we followed it. One turn. Then another. Then another.

. . .

The surroundings never changed.

Same bends. Same trees. Same walls.

The GPS arrow started behaving strangely. Sometimes pointing sideways. Sometimes lagging behind. Sometimes the map jumped ahead like we had teleported.

Then: Rerouting…

Again. And again. And again. One moment straight ahead. A few minutes later a U-turn. Then another reroute. Every suggested route led deeper inside.

. . .

Time also started feeling strange. Could have been 15 minutes. Could have been over 30. Till today, neither of us knows.

The scariest part wasn't even the graveyard. It was the silence. No cars. No people. No sound except our engines. Even the usual late-night insects sounded absent.

The Biker Ahead

Then — headlights. Another biker. Far ahead.

Both of us immediately sped up. Finally, another human.

. . .

I still remember how desperate we felt trying to close the distance. We high-beamed him, waved, pushed harder.

The gap never changed. Not nearer. Not further. Just fixed — no matter how fast we rode.

That was when the panic really started. Because by then, even the GPS had stopped making sense. Map jittering. Arrow spinning randomly. Route line flickering on and off.

. . .

Then, without noticing exactly when, everything changed. Streetlights returned. Cars appeared. Other bikes overtook us. Petrol stations. Shops. Traffic.

Just like that — back on the main road. Like nothing had happened. The biker ahead? Gone. No turning point. No junction. Nothing.

Navigation

Repeated rerouting despite unchanging surroundings
Anomaly 2
Biker ahead — consistent gap, impossible to close
Anomaly 3
Time distortion — estimated 15–30+ mins, unverifiable
Resolution
Sudden normalisation — back on main road, no memory of exit route

What We Understood After

We stopped by the roadside for a while without saying much. Both of us already knew we couldn't explain what had just happened. Maybe bad GPS signal. Maybe too tired, accidentally looping some old road. Maybe the graveyard atmosphere made both of us imagine things we'd rather not name.

But one thing I still remember clearly — that Nokia kept rerouting even when the road in front of us never changed. And that biker ahead never turned around, never pulled away naturally, never disappeared at a visible junction. He was just there. And then he wasn't.

// SIGNAL RECORDED: Repeated rerouting with no navigational logic. Unknown biker ahead maintaining fixed, unclosable distance. Time distortion estimated 15–30+ minutes. No identified exit from the loop. Sudden return to main road with no memory of the transition point.

Additional Field Notes: The Route That Wouldn't End

Log Classification: Unexplained / Navigation Failure / Road Loop

[GPS FAILURE CONTEXT] Early 2000s GPS on consumer phones operated with significant lag, signal dropout, and positional errors — particularly near dense vegetation, old burial grounds, and areas with inconsistent mobile tower coverage. However, repeated rerouting that consistently leads deeper into the same area, rather than outward, is not consistent with standard signal drift behaviour.

[THE BIKER] A vehicle maintaining a perfectly fixed distance regardless of rider speed — not accelerating away, not slowing to allow approach — is not consistent with normal traffic behaviour. The biker did not brake, did not acknowledge high-beam signals, and disappeared without any identifiable junction or turn.

[ROAD LOOPS] Experiences of riding in loops without conscious awareness are documented across night-riding communities in Malaysia and Thailand. The consistent elements: a graveyard or old burial area nearby, GPS failure, loss of time, and a guide figure that cannot be reached.

  • Navigation: Completely unreliable — led inward, not outward
  • Time lost: 15–30+ minutes, unverifiable
  • Recommendation: If the road keeps looping and the GPS keeps rerouting — stop. Wait. Do not follow figures ahead.
Unexplained Log #005

The Night I Ended The Ride Early

Every Friday night for years, I organised a meetup. No registration, no planning — just riders. But this particular night, both the road and my wife were trying to tell me the same thing at the same time.

🌑
// SR-UNX-005 // Friday Night Convoy // Singapore

The Usual Setup

For years, every Friday night was the same. No matter how many riders showed up — five people or fifty — I would organise a meetup somewhere in Singapore. Sometimes Kallang. Sometimes the west side. Sometimes north. Post the location the night before and whoever wanted to ride would just appear. No registration. No planning. Just riders.

That night felt different from the start. Too many people showed up — more than twenty bikes. Some familiar faces, some completely unknown. Usually the mood was noisy: people laughing, revving, talking nonsense. But that night felt strangely heavy. Not silent. Just different. Even the air felt wrong. Still. Humid. Like rain was already waiting somewhere above us.

The Feeling on the Road

We rode the usual route — from the old Kallang KFC area, through town past Cathay Cineleisure Orchard, then north up towards Yishun Dam to rest.

Everything looked normal.

. . .

But the entire night, I had this feeling that something was following the group. Not physically. Just present. Trailing behind us like a shadow that didn't belong to anyone riding.

At first I ignored it. You ride long enough at night, your mind plays tricks — shadows, fatigue, headlights in your mirrors. I told myself it was nothing.

. . .

Then around 11pm, my phone rang. My wife.

"Can you come home earlier tonight?"

I laughed and asked why. She paused before saying: "I don't know… tonight feels wrong."

That immediately hit me. Because I had been feeling the exact same thing the entire ride.

The Decision

I looked around at the group. Big turnout. Dark clouds moving in. Atmosphere getting heavier by the minute.

So for the first time in a long while, I ended the meetup early. "Rain coming already. Go home early tonight."

. . .

Most riders listened and left. But a smaller group stayed behind — they wanted to continue riding to another location after we dismissed.

I headed home.

. . .

Less than thirty minutes later — before I even reached home — my phone started exploding with calls. One after another.

At first I thought maybe someone got stopped by traffic police. Then I answered.

Accident. Bad accident.

The riders who stayed behind had crashed on the way to the next location. Three bikes involved. Two riders survived. But one rider did not come home that night.

What Nobody Said Afterward

Even today, I still cannot forget what I heard from the others who arrived at the scene first. The way his body had come to rest — it wasn't like a normal crash. The position was wrong in a way that stayed in the heads of everyone who saw it.

After that, nobody discussed what happened. No theories. No ghost stories. No arguments about what the night had felt like before it went wrong. Just silence. The kind that means everyone had already decided, quietly and separately, to leave it buried.

What I Still Think About

I still organise rides sometimes. But ever since that night, whenever the atmosphere feels off — heavier than it should be, quieter than the road explains — I go home. No debate. No finishing the route. Just home.

Three things still stay with me. Why did the whole evening feel different before anything had happened? Why did my wife call using almost exactly the words I had been thinking, when she almost never interrupted my Friday rides? And why did I feel genuinely relieved — for the first time ever — after ending a ride early?

// SIGNAL RECORDED: Shared unease between rider and spouse with no prior communication — both described the same feeling independently. Heavy atmospheric sensation throughout the ride preceding the accident. Rider who acted on instinct arrived home safely. Riders who continued did not.

Additional Field Notes: The Night I Ended The Ride Early

Log Classification: Unexplained / Premonition / Fatal Incident

[SHARED PREMONITION] Independent, simultaneous unease felt by a rider and his spouse — with no prior communication — is among the more consistently reported phenomena in accounts preceding serious incidents. The specific language ("tonight feels wrong") appearing almost identically in both the rider's internal experience and the spouse's unprompted call is not easily explained by coincidence alone.

[CONVOY DYNAMICS] Large, informal convoys with unknown riders, mixed skill levels, no formal briefing, and a heavy atmosphere are a combination experienced riders learn to recognise as high risk. The decision to end early in this context was not timid. It was correct.

[THE RIDER TABOO] Among experienced night riders in Singapore and Malaysia, there is a widely understood but rarely spoken rule: never say anything unlucky during a ride, and never ignore the feeling that something is wrong. This is not superstition. It is pattern recognition built from years of witnessing what happens when riders push through a night that is already telling them to stop.

INCIDENT ASSESSMENT
  • Warning signals: Multiple — atmospheric, physical, spousal
  • Outcome for those who left: Safe
  • Outcome for those who stayed: One fatality, two injured
  • Recommendation: When the road tells you to go home — go home.
Unexplained Log #006

The Two Figures At Kent Ridge

Every biker who rode long enough in Singapore eventually heard about the Kent Ridge bends. I heard the stories. I laughed them off. Then one night, I stopped laughing.

🌿
// SR-UNX-006 // Kent Ridge // The 99 Bends

The Road Every Singapore Rider Knows

Every biker who rode long enough in Singapore would eventually hear about the old Kent Ridge bends. Some called it the "99 bends." Some said it was cursed. Some laughed it off completely. The stories were always similar — late at night, riders would claim to see a woman near the sharpest bend. White dress. Long hair. Standing still. Classic ghost-story stuff.

Honestly, I never believed it. I had ridden through so many times it was almost routine. Riders hyped it up like an initiation — "if you ride long enough, sooner or later you'll see something." But every time I went? Nothing. No shadows. No figures. No strange sounds. Just roads, bends, and night air.

The Night I Saw Two

So one night, I rode through again. Faster this time. Road mostly empty. The kind of silence only late-night Singapore carries. Streetlights glowing orange. Trees moving slightly from the wind.

At first everything felt normal.

. . .

Then somewhere near the curvier stretch, I noticed something at the side of the road. A figure. Whitish. Standing near the bend. Not clear enough to fully make out — more like something my eyes caught for a split second while cornering.

. . .

But strangely, I didn't feel fear.

The feeling was calm. Almost protective. Like whatever it was, it wasn't there to harm anyone.

I slowed slightly after the bend. Then further ahead — another figure. Dark. Blackish. Nearer to the staircase area.

. . .

And the feeling from this one was completely different. Heavy. Cold. Not angry.

Just waiting.

What the Difference Meant

That was the part that disturbed me most. Not what I saw — but how completely different both presences felt from each other. One felt like something standing there to protect riders who still had time left. The other felt like something waiting for riders whose time was already up.

At that moment, every old biker story I'd ever laughed at came back. The fatal accidents. The roadside flowers. The riders who never came home. All of it suddenly felt like it had a different weight behind it.

I rolled off the throttle immediately. No more fast riding. No more trying to prove anything. For the first time in my life, I genuinely felt like the road was telling me to stop. So I did. I exited, headed home, and never rode Kent Ridge aggressively again after that night.

Field Data

Location
Kent Ridge Road — late night, mostly empty
Figure 1
Whitish, near the bend — feeling: calm, protective
Figure 2
Dark, near the staircase area — feeling: heavy, waiting
Rider response
Immediate throttle rolloff. Exited. Did not return aggressively.
Prior visits
Multiple — nothing seen previously. This was the first and only sighting.

Maybe it was fatigue. Maybe shadows from the streetlights. But till today, whenever people ask whether the Kent Ridge stories are real, I always give the same answer: "I don't know what I saw. But I know what I felt." And whatever that second presence was waiting for — I had no interest in becoming its reason for being there.

// SIGNAL RECORDED: Two distinct presences at separate points along the same road. Completely different emotional signatures between them — one protective, one anticipatory. Rider compelled to stop. Has not ridden the stretch aggressively since.

Additional Field Notes: The Two Figures At Kent Ridge

Log Classification: Unexplained / Visual Encounter / Location-Based

[KENT RIDGE HISTORY] The Kent Ridge area carries significant wartime history — the Battle of Pasir Panjang in 1942 resulted in heavy casualties along and around the ridge. Accounts of presences along the bends have circulated among Singapore's night riding community for decades, consistently describing two types of sightings near the same locations.

[TWO-PRESENCE PATTERN] The specific distinction — one presence feeling protective and one feeling anticipatory of harm — appears across multiple independent accounts from different riders at different times. That this distinction is consistent, rather than simply generic fear, is notable.

[THE ROAD ITSELF] Kent Ridge Road has been the site of multiple fatal accidents involving motorcycles. The bends are sharp, the road changes character at night, and the canopy reduces both visibility and ambient light. Whether the road is haunted or simply dangerous — both conclusions lead to the same recommendation: ride carefully and leave quickly.

INCIDENT ASSESSMENT
  • Sighting: Two figures, different locations, entirely different emotional signatures
  • Corroborating accounts: Multiple across Singapore riding community
  • Recommendation: Kent Ridge at night is not a proving ground. There is nothing to prove there.
Unexplained Log #007

The Road That Rejected Us

Some rides become memories. Some become lessons. And some feel like the road itself never wanted you there — and kept saying so, one breakdown at a time, until you finally listened.

⚠️
// SR-UNX-007 // Charity Convoy // Alor Setar

The Purpose Behind the Trip

This happened during one of our long-distance charity tours heading towards Laos — carrying funds and supplies for an orphanage. The purpose was good. The intention was right. But from the moment we entered Malaysia, nothing felt right.

The Warnings That Kept Coming

At first it was small things. My bike started developing RPM issues — random drops, unstable revs. We ended up dismantling and washing the carburettor by the roadside in the heat before continuing. Even after fixing it, the bike still didn't feel healthy.

. . .

Then my GPS charging died. The screen slowly dimmed while riding north. Back then GPS wasn't just convenience on long tours — it was confidence. Once the charging went, I suddenly felt blind on unfamiliar roads.

I remember staring at the dimming screen thinking: this trip really doesn't want me here.

. . .

Still, we pushed on. Because riders always push on. You ignore discomfort. You ignore instincts. You convince yourself the destination matters more than the feeling.

The Crash at Alor Setar

Near Alor Setar, everything broke.

A fuel stop. Simple miscommunication. Someone moved unexpectedly. Another misread the direction out.

Then impact.

. . .

Crashes don't happen dramatically. There's no time to react. One second you're riding normally. The next second the road is beside your face.

I crashed hard. When I got up, my bike looked wounded — fairings deeply scratched, signal lights broken off, riding armor torn apart. Even my full-face helmet was heavily scratched.

I remember staring at the helmet longer than the bike. Because riders know what those scratches really mean.

. . .

I didn't shout. Didn't argue. Just took off my gloves and threw them on the ground out of frustration. Because at that moment I knew something invisible had already broken. Not the bike. Me.

Turning Back

Standing there at the roadside, I made the hardest decision of the entire journey. I withdrew. The others continued north towards Laos while I turned back alone towards Ipoh.

But the strange thing — after I withdrew, the trip still continued falling apart. One rider suffered a piston jam. Another self-skidded. One by one, the convoy kept getting hit. That was the moment the feeling fully settled: maybe the road never wanted us to complete this journey from the beginning. Not haunted. Not cursed in the movie sense. Just rejected. Like every warning had already been clearly given — the RPM drops, the failing GPS, the crash, the piston jam, the skid — each one a page of the same message, written in a language riders learn to read the hard way.

After the trip ended, the group went quiet. No arguments. No drama. The group chat became inactive. Nobody brought up the Laos trip again. No one discussed the breakdowns or the crashes or how wrong the entire journey had felt. It was like everyone silently agreed to leave it buried and never go back to it.

Till today, years later, I still occasionally wonder — what exactly was waiting further ahead, if I had never turned back?

Stay In The Light

He only asked one thing the entire ride. Not to slow down. Not to stop. Just: keep me inside the light. I still don't know what he saw in those mirrors.

The Darkness Singapore Riders Don't Prepare For

One thing Singapore riders always notice when crossing into Malaysia for the first time is the darkness. Not fear — just darkness. In Singapore, almost every road is brightly lit. Expressways glow orange through the entire night. Even industrial areas have enough light to see clearly. Malaysia is different. Once you leave the bigger towns, some roads go completely black. No streetlamps. No buildings. No reflections. Only your headlights cutting through emptiness.

That night, a group of us planned a ride specifically for that kind of road. One rider insisted on joining even though he had difficulty seeing at night — in Singapore he was fine, but these roads were different. So I stayed directly in front of him, close enough for him to follow my rear blinkers and the glow from my fog lights.

When His Pace Started Dropping

At first everything was normal. Convoy maintained pace. Engines echoing through the dark.

. . .

Then slowly, I noticed his bike falling further behind. The gap kept increasing. I slowed and detached from the main group to ride beside him. Even through the helmet, his body language looked tense.

"What happened?"

He paused before replying quietly: "Can you stay behind me instead?"

. . .

I told him my fog light was very bright — it would blind him from behind.

He immediately replied: "Doesn't matter."

Then after a few seconds: "As long as I stay inside the light."

. . .

That sentence stayed in my head for years. His tone didn't sound like someone joking. It sounded desperate.

What the Light Did

I moved behind him without asking anything more. The moment my fog lights covered his bike from the rear, he became calmer immediately. Speed stabilised. Riding became smoother. Almost like he was trying to avoid leaving the light completely — like the boundary of that beam was the only safe boundary he had.

. . .

The rest of the journey passed quietly. Nobody discussed anything afterward. He never brought it up again. Not once.

. . .

Then sometime much later, I heard the reason from a mutual rider friend. Apparently during that dark stretch, he believed something was following extremely close behind his bike. Not another vehicle. Not another rider.

Something else.

He never described exactly what he saw. Only that every time he looked into his mirrors, the feeling became stronger. Close enough that he became terrified of crashing before making it back to Singapore.

What I Still Think About

Till today, I still don't know whether he actually saw something in those mirrors, or whether the darkness simply played tricks on an already anxious mind. Both explanations are possible. Both feel incomplete.

What I know is this: he never asked me to slow down, never asked to stop, never asked to turn back. Only one request the entire ride. Keep me inside the light. Said with the kind of quiet urgency you only use when you genuinely believe something is right behind you.

The Two Hotels That Got Worse

Long-distance touring teaches you that the cheapest hotels often leave the strongest memories. Thailand gave us two — each stranger than the last — and by the second one, nobody was pretending to be brave anymore.

🏚️
// SR-TOUR-001 // Thailand Touring // 380 Baht & The Mountain

The 380-Baht Hotel

During long-distance touring, riders eventually learn one thing: the cheapest hotels create the strongest memories. This night came after an already draining stretch — we had been through an accident the day before, so everyone was running on empty. Body tired. Mind tired. Bikes dirty. Mood heavy. By the time we found a place, nobody cared about quality. 380 baht. Cheap enough that we accepted without thinking much.

At first glance, the place looked less like a hotel and more like an old army barrack. Long corridors. Plain walls. Dim lighting. Even climbing the stairs felt familiar in an uncomfortable way — it instantly reminded me of Tekong during NS.

. . .

The moment we entered the room, something felt wrong. Not dirty. Not obviously haunted-looking. Just wrong. The room was unusually large for such a cheap hotel. Too much empty space. That kind of silence where even your own footsteps sound unnaturally loud.

We switched on the TV immediately. Then all the room lights. Nobody suggested turning anything off afterward. That itself already said enough.

. . .

At one point I stepped outside alone for air. The corridor was dim and completely empty. No footsteps. No voices. Just the distant humming of old building lights.

And standing there, I suddenly had this overwhelming feeling that something was nearby. Not visible. Not dramatic. Just the strong instinct that I shouldn't stay outside too long.

I went straight back inside and closed the door.

That night, none of us switched off the lights. The TV stayed on until morning. And despite being physically exhausted, sleep never felt deep.

The Mountain Hotel

That 380-baht place was strange enough. But there was another hotel later in the same trip that turned out even worse — bad enough that all of us ended up sleeping together in the same room just to get through the night.

We didn't choose it because it looked good. We chose it because it was the nearest available place after riding past nightfall. And once night fully settles on those mountain roads in Thailand, everything changes. The route leading in was almost completely dark. No streetlamps. No shops. No proper signboards.

Even finding the hotel entrance was difficult. When we finally entered the compound, we couldn't immediately find the owner — he was sitting somewhere deeper inside the darkness with a few other guests. That alone already felt unsettling.

. . .

After some awkward communication, we were told only two rooms were left. But the rooms were separated far apart. One was located further inside the compound, deeper into the darkness. The other was nearer to a wooden fence near the entrance.

The moment we heard that, both of us made the same decision without discussion.

One room. Together. No point acting brave.

. . .

The room itself barely felt like a proper hotel. Old structure — concrete, wood panels, metal roofing. The kind where every movement creates sound. The TV was a CRT — huge fat back, tiny screen. But it became comforting. Not for watching. Just for noise. Something to push against the silence outside.

. . .

Then we discovered the toilet. To reach it, we had to lower our heads through a small gate-like opening, then step partially outside into darkness before reaching the toilet a few steps away. Beyond that — just wilderness. No proper walls. Just darkness and nature.

Nobody wanted to go alone.

. . .

I was knocked out from exhaustion and didn't hear anything that night. Only the next morning did my riding buddy tell me what happened.

Sometime very late, the electricity tripped. The room went dark. Then the old CRT turned to static — that loud, old-school crackling sound. He woke up, found the switch, restored power, then quickly turned the TV off.

And according to him — the moment the electricity came back on, everything suddenly felt normal again. No more strange atmosphere. No more uneasy feeling. Like whatever had been pressing against the room had decided to leave when the lights returned.

What Both Hotels Had in Common

Till today, I still don't know whether either experience was just exhaustion and unfamiliar surroundings, or something the walls of those places were holding from before we arrived. Both explanations feel possible. Both feel incomplete.

But one detail from the mountain hotel still sticks. Out of all the rooms available that night — neither of us chose the one deeper inside the darkness. We made that decision instantly, without discussion, without needing to explain it to each other. Some choices don't need reasoning. The instinct is fast enough on its own.

Not threatening. Just strangely still.

Familiarity.

Not the kind where you recognise a place from photos online. The uncomfortable kind — where your mind already knows the environment before you've explored it. Comfort. The room felt familiar in a way that made no sense.

No "first-time" feeling. Just an overwhelming sense that I had returned somewhere, rather than arrived somewhere new.

. . .

The weirdest part is that nothing paranormal happened that night. No shadows. No strange noises. No sightings. Everything was peaceful. We slept well, woke up normally, and continued our journey the next morning without any issues.

Out of every hotel I've stayed at across all my touring trips, that is still the only place that never felt unfamiliar to me. Not even on arrival. Not even in the first few seconds.

I've tried to explain it rationally — similar layouts, exhaustion warping perception, pattern recognition from years of budget hotels. All possible. None of them fully account for knowing where the light switches were before I reached for them. Or for the settled, returned feeling that was there from the moment I stepped through the door.

Some places feel like you've been there before. This one felt like I had left something there and was coming back to collect it. I still don't know what that means. And honestly, I'm not sure I want to.

No explanation for the time. No memory of stopping. No detour. Just four hours on a two-hour road, always following something ahead that never waited for us to catch up.

Every day, I was told to place KitKats and Vitagen somewhere inside the house.

And strangely, I never questioned it.

To me back then, it felt completely normal. Almost routine. Like feeding fish or watering plants. Just: put the snacks there. And I would do it, obediently, without any fear or hesitation.

What Actually Disturbs Me Now

That's the disturbing part when I think back on it today. Not the ghost story itself. But how natural the entire situation felt to a child. No fear. No panic. No feeling that anything was wrong. Just acceptance — as if the arrangement had always existed and I had simply been told my part in it.

Till today, I still don't know whether the owner genuinely believed something was living in that house, whether my childhood imagination created invisible companions, or whether the adults around me unknowingly shaped my memories into something they weren't.

But one detail quietly disturbs me every time I think about it. I cannot remember the playing at all. Not a single image. Not a single moment. Only the offerings. KitKats and Vitagen. Every single day. And the older I get, the stranger that missing memory feels — like something was deliberately taken out of reach, and the only proof it existed is the gap it left behind.
